#412 Tell me about a time you failed

Medium Behavioral

The Question

"Tell me about a time when you failed at something."

Alternative phrasings you might encounter:

  • "Describe a project or situation that didn't go as planned."
  • "What's your biggest professional failure?"
  • "Tell me about a time you made a mistake."
  • "Describe a situation where you failed to meet expectations."

Key Topics to Cover

  • The Situation: Set the context - what were you trying to accomplish?
  • The Failure: What went wrong? Be specific and honest
  • Your Responsibility: Own your part - don't make excuses
  • The Lessons: What did you learn from this experience?
  • The Growth: How did this change your approach going forward?
  • Redemption (if applicable): Did you get a chance to apply these lessons successfully?

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Don't blame others: Taking responsibility shows maturity
Avoid trivial examples: "I was 5 minutes late once" won't impress
Don't end on the failure: Always pivot to lessons learned and growth
Skip the humble-brag: "I work too hard" isn't a real failure

STAR Method for Behavioral Questions

Structure your answer using the STAR framework to provide a clear, compelling narrative:

S

Situation

Set the scene - provide context about the project, team, and environment

  • What was the project or initiative?
  • Who was involved?
  • What were the expectations?
  • When did this occur?
‚è±Ô∏è Aim for: 20-30 seconds
T

Task

Explain your specific responsibility and what you were trying to achieve

  • What was your role?
  • What were you trying to accomplish?
  • What were the goals or objectives?
  • What challenges did you face?
‚è±Ô∏è Aim for: 15-20 seconds
A

Action

Describe what you did, where you went wrong, and how you responded

  • What actions did you take?
  • Where did things go off track?
  • What was your mistake or oversight?
  • How did you respond when you realized the failure?
‚è±Ô∏è Aim for: 40-60 seconds
R

Result

Share the outcome, lessons learned, and how you grew from the experience

  • What was the ultimate outcome?
  • What lessons did you learn?
  • How did you change your approach afterward?
  • Have you successfully applied these lessons since?
‚è±Ô∏è Aim for: 30-40 seconds
Total time target: 2-3 minutes

For failure questions, spend more time on the Result (lessons and growth) than the Action (the failure itself)

Expert Tips

‚úÖ

Choose a Real, Meaningful Failure

Pick something significant enough to show vulnerability but not so catastrophic that it raises red flags. A good failure story shows:

  • You've faced challenges and learned from them
  • You can admit mistakes without being defensive
  • You actively work on self-improvement
üí¨

Use Specific Details

Vague answers lack credibility. Include:

  • Concrete numbers (team size, deadline, impact)
  • Specific actions you took
  • Measurable outcomes
  • Real emotions you felt
üéØ

Focus on the Takeaway

The failure itself is just setup - the real story is what you learned. Demonstrate:

  • Clear understanding of what went wrong and why
  • Specific changes you made to your approach
  • Evidence of applying these lessons successfully
  • Ongoing commitment to growth
üöÄ

End with Redemption

If possible, share how you later succeeded in a similar situation by applying your lessons:

  • "Since then, I've successfully delivered 5 projects on time by..."
  • "I applied this lesson when I... and achieved..."
  • "This experience shaped how I approach..."

Practice Questions

Prepare similar answers for these related questions:

  • "Tell me about a time you made a mistake."
  • "Describe your biggest professional failure."
  • "Tell me about a project that didn't go as planned."
  • "What's something you would do differently if you could go back?"
  • "Tell me about a time you received negative feedback."

Sample Answer

Situation

"In my previous role as a software engineer at TechCorp, I was leading the development of a major feature for our mobile app. We had a tight 6-week deadline to launch before our biggest marketing campaign. The feature would allow users to share content directly to social media, which our user research showed was highly requested."

Task

"As the technical lead, I was responsible for architecting the solution, coordinating with a team of 3 other developers, and ensuring we met the deadline. I needed to deliver a robust, scalable solution that could handle our expected traffic spike."

Action

"I designed what I thought was an elegant solution, but I made a critical mistake - I didn't involve the team early enough in the architecture decisions. I spent the first week working alone on the design, thinking I was being efficient. When I presented my approach to the team, they raised several concerns about edge cases I hadn't considered and integration challenges with our existing systems."

"Rather than taking their feedback seriously and reworking the design, I dismissed their concerns because I was worried about losing time. I pushed forward with my original plan. As a result, we ran into exactly the problems they predicted. Three weeks in, we had to stop and completely redesign our approach. The team was frustrated, and we missed our deadline by two weeks."

Result

"The feature eventually launched successfully, but missing the deadline meant we weren't ready for the marketing campaign, which cost the company significant opportunity. More importantly, I had damaged trust with my team by not listening to their input."

"This failure taught me three critical lessons: First, collaborative design catches problems early and leads to better solutions. Second, experienced team members' concerns deserve serious consideration, even when you're under pressure. Third, taking time upfront to do things right saves time overall."

"I completely changed how I approach technical leadership. Now, I involve the team from day one in architecture decisions. I've since led 5 major projects where we delivered on time by leveraging collective expertise. I also implemented design review sessions at my current company, which has reduced rework by about 30%. That painful experience made me a much more effective technical leader."

Why This Answer Works

  • Authentic: Shows real vulnerability without being catastrophic
  • Ownership: Takes clear responsibility without blaming the team
  • Specific: Includes concrete details (timeline, team size, impact)
  • Learning: Articulates clear, actionable lessons
  • Growth: Demonstrates how behavior changed afterward
  • Impact: Shows positive results from applying lessons (5 projects, 30% reduction)

Another Example - Failed Launch

Situation: Product Manager launching new feature based on assumptions

Task: Increase user engagement by 20%

Action: Built feature without sufficient user research; launched to lukewarm response

Result: Only 5% adoption; learned importance of user validation; now runs user testing before building; next feature hit 35% adoption in first month
